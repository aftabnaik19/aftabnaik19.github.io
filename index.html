What Just Happened

Why does luck matter even when skill dominates?

In any large population, the most skilled people are bunched closely together at the top. When thousands compete, the difference between the 99th and 99.9th percentile of skill is tiny. But luck varies independently - and when you stack even a small luck bonus on top of near-identical skill, luck becomes the tiebreaker.

This is known as "The Paradox of Skill." As overall skill levels rise and converge, luck plays an increasingly large role in determining who ends up on top. Coined by Michael Mauboussin, this concept explains why the most successful outcomes - in business, sports, academia - almost always involve someone who was both talented and lucky.

The key insight is structural, not moral. It doesn't mean winners didn't earn it - it means that among many deserving candidates, randomness tipped the scales. The larger the population, the more extreme this effect becomes.

About The Luck Engine

This simulation generates a population of people, each with random skill and luck values. Their final score is a weighted combination of both. You control the weight - how much luck matters vs. skill.

Even when skill weight is 95% or higher, the top performers almost always had unusually high luck. This isn't a fluke - it's a mathematical certainty in large populations where skill compresses at the top.

Inspired by the work of Michael Mauboussin on "The Paradox of Skill" and research on success, talent, and randomness.

Built as an educational tool. No data is collected. Everything runs in your browser.
